The April 2008 issue of the[*Journal of the Association for History and Computing*](http://quod.lib.umich.edu/j/jahc/browse.html) highlights the use of digitized primary source documents in the classroom and in the archives.
[*The Historical Scene Investigation Project: Facilitating Historical Thinking with Web-Based, Digital Primary Source Documents*](http://quod.lib.umich.edu/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=jahc;idno=3310410.0011.101;cc=jahc;rgn=main;view=text) uses the case approach to explore the use of digital information in the context of a project for teaching history in the K-12 classroom.
[*The Importance of Context for Digitized Archival Collections*](http://quod.lib.umich.edu/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=jahc;idno=3310410.0011.102;cc=jahc;rgn=main;view=text) talks about the consequences of selective digitization. How do we read a document when we don't know what came before or after it in a collection?
Editor Jeremy Boggs, [highlights online exhibitions](http://quod.lib.umich.edu/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=jahc;idno=3310410.0011.104;cc=jahc;rgn=main;view=text), and editor Lynn Westney [takes a look at E-Journals](http://quod.lib.umich.edu/cgi/t/text/text-idx?c=jahc;idno=3310410.0011.105;cc=jahc;rgn=main;view=text).
