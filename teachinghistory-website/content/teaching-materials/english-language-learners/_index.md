Students learning English face dual challenges in history classrooms: mastering historical content while acquiring language skills. Yet history instruction offers rich opportunities for English Language Learners (ELLs) to develop academic language through meaningful, content-driven engagement.

Drawing on the research of Stanford professor Claude Goldenberg, we have identified three research-based guidelines and gathered resources to help history teachers support their English Language Learners.

## 1. Using Materials in the First Language

"Teaching students in their first language promotes the acquisition of knowledge and skills that can be applied in English." Strategies include teaching skills in the primary language, previewing lessons in students' native languages, and providing multilingual resources.

## 2. Scaffolding Instruction

"Teachers must modify instruction to help ELL students access important knowledge and skills." Examples include graphic organizers, presenting information both verbally and visually, and clarifying key vocabulary.

## 3. Good Teaching for All

"What you know about good history teaching also applies to teaching ELLs." Effective practices include content-rich environments, an interactive curriculum, clear objectives, opportunities for application, and constructive feedback with parent communication.
