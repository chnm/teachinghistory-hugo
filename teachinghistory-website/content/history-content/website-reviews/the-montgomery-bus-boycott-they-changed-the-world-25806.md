This [website](http://web.archive.org/web/20220127214813/http://www.montgomeryboycott.com/) is a detailed look at the Montgomery Bus Boycott of 1955–1956 as covered in the *Montgomery Advertiser*. It includes more than 20 oral histories, more than 40 archival news stories, and more than 20 images of the event. A 3,000-word [overview](http://web.archive.org/web/20220127214813/http://web.archive.org/web/20201105231646/http://www.montgomeryboycott.com/overview/) section provides details regarding the boycott, the people involved, why it occurred, and the outcome. A [biography](http://web.archive.org/web/20220127214813/http://web.archive.org/web/20160216070626/http://www.montgomeryboycott.com:80/biographies/) section includes more details regarding the individuals involved with the boycott. A [memorial page](http://web.archive.org/web/20220127214813/http://web.archive.org/web/20201114004042/http://www.montgomeryboycott.com/rosa-parks/) for Rosa Parks, one of the most visible figures of the Montgomery Bus Boycott, includes photos, videos, and news articles from the *Montgomery Advertiser* regarding her death. "[Voices of the Boycott](http://web.archive.org/web/20220127214813/http://web.archive.org/web/20160214112336/http://www.montgomeryboycott.com:80/voices-of-the-boycott/)" includes oral histories from the participants of the boycott.

The site also includes an archive of stories from the *Montgomery Advertiser* and the Associated Press regarding the boycott. This includes front page stories, as well as smaller news stories from within the newspaper. Finally a [photo gallery](http://web.archive.org/web/20220127214813/http://web.archive.org/web/20160216070642/http://www.montgomeryboycott.com:80/portfolio1_categories/photos/) contains booking photos, images of speeches, and photos of the events. None of the images in the gallery are graphic in nature.

Though the site is very user-friendly and all information easily accessible, it is somewhat limited in its focus on primary sources directly tied to the *Montgomery Advertiser*. Still, this website could potentially be useful for educators and students looking to supplement their studies on the U.S. Civil Rights Movement, especially due to the inclusion of the oral histories.
