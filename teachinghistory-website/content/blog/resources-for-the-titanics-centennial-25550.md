April 15, 2012, marks the centennial of the sinking of the *RMS Titanic*, a disaster that still looms large in popular imagination. Why is the *Titanic* so well remembered? What sets it apart from the hundreds of disasters at sea that have happened before and after?

Use the centennial as a chance to ask your students how they know what they know about history. Do they know about the ship? Where did they learn about it? From a book or a movie? Your students can bring together the skills of historians and scientists to uncover their *own* answers about the disaster—from who was on the ship to why it sank to how the story of the *Titanic* fits into history and the present day.

Where to begin? We've gathered some ideas for places to start your search for resources:

* Research the *Titanic* with the Library of Congress's [guide](http://www.loc.gov/rr/program/bib/titanic/Titanic.html) to Library resources. The Library's [blog for teachers](http://blogs.loc.gov/teachers/2012/04/the-titanic-in-the-news-and-in-memory/) also rounds up useful primary sources and links to lesson plans.
* Discover primary sources buried in the collections of the National Archives and Records Administration (NARA). NARA blog entries look at [letters](http://blogs.archives.gov/TextMessage/2012/02/27/browsing-serendipity-and-a-titanic-discovery/) sent to President Taft about the sinking, one survivor's [luggage ticket and coupon](http://blogs.archives.gov/prologue/?p=8644), and [compensation claims](http://blogs.archives.gov/prologue/?p=8460) from the widows of postal clerks. (NARA also holds a [1912](http://www.archives.gov/research/immigration/titanic-survivors-to-ny.html) list of some of the survivors of the disaster.)
* Interested in learning more about the *Titanic*'s postal clerks? Get to know them with the National Postal Museum's [exhibit](http://www.postalmuseum.si.edu/titanic/) *Posted Aboard* RMS Titanic. All of the clerks died at sea, determined to rescue the ship's mail.
* View artifacts related to the voyage and disaster from the [Smithsonian Institution.](http://www.si.edu/encyclopedia_si/nmah/titanic.htm)
* Read articles related to the disaster and share teaching ideas on the *New York Times Learning Network* [feature](http://learning.blogs.nytimes.com/2012/04/03/100-years-later-ways-to-teach-about-the-titanic-with-the-times/?nl=learning&emc=edit_lpmo_20120403).
* Get a UK perspective on the ship courtesy of the [BBC](http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/17488357), including survivor interviews and an interactive look at the wreck site.
* Learn about the science and history behind the story of the *Titanic* with resources from [National Geographic Education](http://education.nationalgeographic.com/education/search/?token=titanic&searchSubmit.x=0&searchSubmit.y=0&page_num=1&per_page=10&sorting=0&tab=0&ar_a=1#page_num=1&sorting=0&per_page=10&tab=0&token=). A [lesson plan](http://www.nationalgeographic.com/xpeditions/lessons/18/g68/ballardsleuthing.html) from National Geographic Xpeditions connects geography and history, and "[Unseen *Titanic*](http://ngm.nationalgeographic.com/2012/04/titanic/sides-text)," an interactive web feature includes zoomable images of the wreck.
* Navigate the creation and sinking of the *Titanic* with [HISTORY.com's](http://www.history.com/topics/titanic/interactives/titanic-interactive) interactives, videos, and photos.
